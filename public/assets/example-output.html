In a step forward for patients with a rare genetic condition, scientists have shown that long-term use of a medication called bevacizumab can help maintain hearing and control tumor growth in people with NF2-related schwannomatosis (NF2-SWN). This condition, previously known as neurofibromatosis type 2, causes benign (non-cancerous) tumors to grow in the nervous system, including the vestibular schwannomas—tumors that form on the nerves responsible for hearing and balance. These tumors often lead to hearing loss, ringing in the ears (tinnitus), and other serious complications.

The study, published in Neuro-Oncology in August 2023, represents a significant step in understanding how to manage NF2-SWN. Researchers conducted a prospective phase II clinical trial—a type of study that looks at the effectiveness and safety of a treatment. The trial focused on maintenance therapy with bevacizumab, a drug that blocks a protein called vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). VEGF is known to promote the growth of blood vessels, which tumors need to thrive. By inhibiting this protein, bevacizumab can help shrink or stabilize tumors and improve symptoms like hearing loss.

The Innovation: Targeted Maintenance Therapy

What makes this study stand out is its focus on “maintenance therapy.” After an initial higher-dose treatment phase, participants received a lower dose of bevacizumab every three weeks for 18 months. The idea was to strike a balance—keeping the tumors and symptoms under control while minimizing side effects that can arise with higher doses.

Findings: Hope for Hearing and Tumor Stability

The results were encouraging. Among the 20 participants, most of whom were young adults, the majority maintained their hearing over the 18-month period. Specifically, 95% of participants had stable hearing after 48 weeks, and 70% remained stable at 98 weeks. Tumor growth was also controlled in 89% of cases by the end of the study.

Importantly, the treatment also had a positive impact on quality of life. Many participants reported less distress from tinnitus and maintained their overall ability to perform daily activities. The medication was generally well tolerated, with only a small number of participants discontinuing due to side effects such as high blood pressure or mild kidney issues.

Significance: A Step Forward for NF2-SWN

NF2-SWN is a challenging condition. People who develop it are often diagnosed at a young age and face lifelong medical challenges. Hearing loss can be particularly devastating, affecting communication and quality of life. Bevacizumab’s ability to preserve hearing and slow tumor growth offers new hope to these patients and their families. This study is also significant because it demonstrates the potential of targeted therapy. Unlike traditional treatments such as surgery or radiation, which can carry significant risks and complications, bevacizumab offers a less invasive way to manage the disease.

Challenges and Limitations

Despite these promising results, there are limitations to the study. The small number of participants means that larger studies are needed to confirm these findings. Additionally, the drug appeared to be less effective in children, who showed higher rates of tumor growth and hearing decline compared to adults. This raises questions about how age and other factors might influence the treatment’s effectiveness.

There are also concerns about the long-term safety of bevacizumab. While the side effects observed in this study were manageable, prolonged use of the drug can lead to issues like high blood pressure and kidney problems. Researchers stress the need for ongoing monitoring and further studies to determine the best dosing strategies and duration of treatment.

The Bigger Picture

The success of this trial is part of a broader shift in medicine toward precision therapies—treatments tailored to the specific needs of individual patients. For those with NF2-SWN, this approach could mean fewer surgeries, better preservation of hearing, and improved quality of life. While challenges remain, the study’s findings bring new optimism to the fight against this rare but serious condition.

As scientists continue to explore bevacizumab and other potential therapies, one thing is clear: advances in targeted treatments are not just changing lives; they are changing what’s possible.
